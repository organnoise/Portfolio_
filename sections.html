About

I'm an artist, musician, and creative technologist from Toronto, ON.

I studied Integrated Media at OCAD University (BFA, 2013); where I made sonic art, animations, short films, silkscreen and lithographic prints, websites, and kinetic sculptures. Through the OCADU exchange program I was able to study in the School of the Museum of Fine Arts' Sonic Arts program and study advanced Max/MSP development in Berklee College of Music's Electronic Production and Design department. I spent a year in OCADU's remote studios in Florence, IT, where I wrote music and built a robotic record player from parts primarily scavenged from e-waste.

I then studied Music Technology at the California Institute of the Arts (MFA, 2017) where I did more of the same, but in addition I studied music composition, embedded system design, creative coding, and analog/digital music production techniques.

My work and research can be broken into three areas: I build instruments and tools for artist expression; write, record and produce music personally and professionally with other artists; and make artwork reflecting on my thoughts about consumer culture, and our relationship with media.

MO: Mobile Objects Development Platform

2017 - 2018

Info:

The Mobile Objects Development Platform is a collection of hardware and software tools for developing mobile instruments. This is a project led by Ali Momeni in Carnegie Mellon University's ArtFab Lab. I worked on the project as research assistant developing both the hardware and software for the project with Ali.

This development platform was implemented into two pre-existing projects by Ali to develop new instruments. MOD: Mobile Object for Drawing a portable, multi-channel video looper outfitted with a battery-powered projector. And MOM: Mobile Object for Music, a portable electronic music instrument that samples live input and manipulates it with grainular synthesis. Both projects use a Raspberry Pi to run openFrameworks and PureData respectively.

Key features of the development kit include:

  • Midi I/O control
  • 12 Analog Inputs, 2 JoyStick Inputs, 16 switch Inputs
  • 16 PWM(External Voltage Supply), 5 Digital Outs
  • 2 Servo Outputs, 2 DC Motor Control Outputs
  • Generative .H, .XML, and .txt files based on Eagle schematics syncing pinouts and coresponding Midi-info for Arduino, openFrameworks, and Pd
  • PureData abstractions aware of the hardware's Midi settings
  • LiPo Battery Powered

2016 - 2017

Info:

Art Arcade was a group show I organized and led with Niels Henrik Bugge and Ana Pérez López spanning the Spring 2017 semester at CalArts. The show focused on the use of arcade-entertainment technology, most specifically the use of coin mechanisms. Nine artists participated in the show including the three project leaders and were chosen after an open-call for proposals. The project was sponsored by both the CalArts Interdisciplinary Project Grant and CalArts Student Union Project Grants which afforded us the oppurtunity to purchase numerous vending machines and media supplies from which we could create with.

I acted as the primary technical coordinator, helping the integration of the coin-mechanism into any project that required one as well as additional development of an openFrameworks app for CalArts Lobby Remote Video Content Release Mechanism's' remote video player system and mechanical engineering for Peep Show.

The critical aim of Art Arcade was to present a platform to question the conventions surrounding interaction with art at present; exploring the potential for work to exist at the intersection between high and low culture.

The implementation of arcade technology, specifically coin-operated mechanisms, is an attempt to rethink the typical form of interaction with gallery-based artwork; coin-operated pieces are a novel occurrence in contemporary art. The coin-mechanism was chosen to be the primary interface to create art from as it represents the essential experience from arcades that the author wished to appropriate into a fine art context: a small monetary sum in exchange for an experience. Placing this exchange in the forefront of each piece presents the audience with the chance to consider how the value of an artistic experience is applied, and if it is altered by the monetary value associated with it.

From the pairing of the high culture associated with fine art and the low culture associated with arcades, it was the hope of the author that unconventional modes of thinking and art-making would be encouraged. The Art Arcade project aimed to convolve these two perceptions and form a synthetic, cross cultural understanding and appreciation for art as a whole. In addition to this crossing of cultures, arcade technology presented a collection of technical and conceptual initial working points from which artists could draw inspiration from.

Works:

Double Dipper, Noah Malone:

Double Dipper is a mechanism that automates the action of dipping tortilla chips into salsa. Users are presented with a set of instructions explaining how to prep the machine by clipping in two chips to either side of the machine, and then activating the dipping process with a quarter. When dipping has concluded, users may eat the chips or pay an additional quarter to continue dipping the chip to their liking.

Free Medical Dispenser (Tool of Weaponry and Resistance to the Medical-Industrial Complex, Molly Surazhsky:

Free Medical Dispenser examines border relations and healthcare rights. A gumball machine that only accepts Mexican pesos was filled with medication purchased in Tijuana. The medication was free to visitors; after signing a liability form they were granted a peso to retrieve medication.

Surazhsky explains in her proposal that “Americans visit Tijuana daily to receive prescription drugs for much cheaper and reasonable prices. In an act of resistance towards the domination and market profitability of Big Pharma, I propose to challenge through the act of illegality, Americans’ access to medicine and extend the notion that healthcare is a human right. Additionally, by crossing the border, I want to interrogate the rise of neonationalism in the act of moving across and between borders.”

The Existentialist Bubble Gum Dispenser, Hande Sever:

Sever explains in her proposal that “The Existentialist Bubble Gum Dispenser is a bubble gum dispenser that vends bubble gums wrapped with existentialist texts inspired by works of Sartre, de Beauvoir and Camus. These texts will be based on the listed authors’ works written as a response to the horrors of Nazi occupation of France such as The Blood of Others, Dirty Hands and The Myth of Sisyphus. Because of the politics of contemporary memory manipulation, we have become accustomed to spitting out disturbing news and history from our minds as we would spit out a bubble gum when its taste goes away; therefore, Your Existentialist Bubble Gum Vendor references our daily habit of forgetting while reminding us of the past horrors."

Loplop Drives, Jack Taylor:

Loplop Drives is a gumball machine that dispensed copies of Jack Taylor’s book Loplop on USB stick. As the book was originally distributed in a limited number of prints, Taylor was interested in the potential for digital distribution with the gumball dispenser being the starting point.

Taylor on the project:
"The central critical goal of this particular text is to portray a theory of recombination, or meaning that is developed through an editorial process of de- and reconstructing material to form temporary relations of significance. Through the alternation between poetics and criticality, the work establishes a conceptual architecture, making readers generate meaning by assuming the poetic model presented to us by the metaphorical Charles Guiteau: a dead and delirious voice animated to negotiate a schizophrenic topography. Charles Guiteau represents a figure that is alien enough, and yet real enough in the historical sense, to act as a malleable conduit between the expressive tropes of the past and whatever weird beasts are created in the future"

Sanctioned (Out of Order), Roksana Pirouzmand:

Sanctioned (Out of Order) examines the relationships between the United States and the six countries sanctioned by it; Iran, Cuba, The Ivory Coast, North Korea, Burma, and Syria. What should be a sanctioned country currency exchange machine is “Out of Order.” This appropriation of the term commonly associated with broken machinery communicates to the audience the lack of interaction possible with these countries, and therefore lack of interaction possible with the piece.

Phenakistoscope, Ana Pérez López:

Phenakistoscope is an experimental animation project which syncs a record player’s RPM and the frame rate of mobile phone’s camera to create an animated loop from a spinning disk of separate animation frames. Instructions prompted users to take out their phones, set them to camera mode, insert a coin, and enjoy. On insertion of a coin the record player’s motor and a lamp illuminating the animation engaged, allowing users to investigate the activated animation with their mobile phone.

CalArts Lobby Remote Video Content Release Mechanism, Gavin Mottram:

CalArts Lobby Remote Video Content Release Mechanism consists of a coin-operated video controller installed in the main space of the Art Arcade and a video gallery installed on a different floor of CalArts. When users insert a coin the red light on the video controller turns off to indicate that a new video is playing in the lobby of CalArts.

Users have the option to run downstairs to try and catch the video, coordinate a system with a partner in which one person pays and the other person watches, activate a video for strangers in the lobby, or to be confused as to what they just paid for. An open call for submissions was made by Mottram asking for short video pieces or found footage evoking a sense of obscurity and the feeling of channel surfing.

Peepshow, Niels Henrik Bugge:

Peep Show reveals a meticulously crafted mask for a fee. A curtain is lifted for a short time to allow the viewer to see the piece. Upon multiple viewings the viewer may realize the system only seems to reveal the mask when it's less meticulously-crafted side is visible.

Claw Machine, Daniel McNamara:

Claw Machine is a commercial claw machine filled with porcelain and glassware. Users are invited to interact with the piece just as they would typically with a claw machine, the only difference being that a prize won will inevitably be destroyed from the drop into the prize bin of the machine.

Roulette: Generative Drum Sequencer

2015 - 2018

Info:

Roulette is a generative sequencer that uses probability algorithms to create rhythmically varied drum patterns. Compositionally the intent of Roulette is to enable its user to program drum patterns in a dynamic fashion, as opposed to the static nature of typical sequential composition. From the initial design of a rhythm, varied patterns are emitted outlining a principal musical form.

Roulette was developed as part of my thesis work during my time at CalArts. All hardware, software and enclosures were developed by myself. Roulette began as purely software and then the physical instrument came afterwards as it was determined that interfacing with the software would benefit from a custom controller.

Ticklers

2016

Info:

Ticklers is an on-going project based out the ArtFab Lab of Carnegie Mellon University by Ali Momeni. The essential goal of Ticklers is to create a portable, user-friendly electroacoustic instrument. In its current state a tickler is a small electro-acoustic instrument that consists of a 3D printed body capable of attaching to a mobile phone, household noise generating items (hair-ties, screws), a piezo-pickup paired with a high-gain preamp, and software designed for both iOS and Android that manipulates the audio signal received from the hardware.

More information on the project can be found here on Ali's website.

The Concourse of Messages

2015 - 2016

Info:

The Concourse of Messages is an interactive installation that I collaborated on with Shih-lien Yen, Daniel Gower, and Ming-huei Huang. I was responsible for developing the interactive elements of the project. Using a combination of Max 7, Arduino, and Raspberry Pi I created a non-linear video environment that enabled Shih-lien Yen's animations of postcards to be triggered when audience members interacted with the postcards on display in the center of the installation room.

"Postcards form connections between people through messages of emotion as they travel in space and time. The Concourse of Messages is the union station where they come to life while waiting for departure, as well as bridging the virtual and physical world through an interactive multimedia experience." - Shih-lien Yen

Experimental Print

CMYK Spray Paint Halftone

2014

Info:

The purpose of this experiment was to see the graphic quality of halftones when printed with a laser cut stencil and spray paint. Using a laser cutter, 4 halftone-vectors from a CMYK separation were cut into separate stencils.

I was interested in this process as a possible alternative to CMYK silk-screening; acting as a potential solution to the difficulty that silk-screening has with printing onto contoured surfaces as well as a way to easily make CMYK prints with a minimal amount of tools and studio space.

Sunset In a Box

2013

Info:

Sunset In A Box is an RGB lighting system that emulates a sunset. I made it for my Aunt and Uncle to light their ceiling that I had painted a sky onto 2 years earlier.

The system uses a Raspberry Pi running Satellite CCRMA, Samba, Pure Data, and an Arduino micro. The user communicates with it using Touch OSC over Wi-Fi to a PD patch running on the Pi that instructs the lights what actions to perform. The touch OSC interface has an on/off button, dimmer, a colour wheel that scrubs through all the preset colours I chose as fitting sunset colours, 3 faders for manual RGB manipulation, and a button for auto-sunset mode that runs an animation of the sunset colours on a loop in 15 minute intervals.

This project was expanded to incorporate lighting control via HTTP requests as a collaboration with UCIC, a startup I worked for, to demonstrate the Ubi’s HTTP request capabilities. I created a video for the project demoing how I added a Node.js program to the lighting system’s Raspberry Pi that triggers different OSC messages to the PD patch when certain HTTP requests are made, through an Ubi voice command to the Pi.

Yo Yo Machine

2011

Info:

The Yo-Yo Machine is semi-automatic; it works by using a cam to lower and raise a lever that is anchored with an elastic band. When the cam has a drastic change in height the elastic recoils and brings the lever arm with it. The yo-yo’s string is attached to the lever arm and is held in place by two rubber stirring-spoons attached to a bike break until the user decides to drop the yo-yo and turn the cam to retrieve it.

Mad Barrowsky

2013

Info:

Mad Barrowsky is a moniker under which I released a series of songs I made in 2012 -2013. Mad Barrowsky is a marrowsky of Bad Marrowsky. “If You’ve Got Some Time… Could You Check Out My Demo?” is the first album I have written and recorded as Mad Barrowsky.

I chose to release the music onto 8-track cartridges as a parody of a trend in independent music of releasing music on cassette tape. Releasing the album onto 8-track represents to me the idea of self-sabotage by rendering my artwork nearly inaccessible, the title of the album is a nod to this. Whenever handing a copy of the album on 8-track to someone the response tends to be that the person would like to listen to it but they don’t know where to find an 8-track player.

The 8-Track was installed with a player for two shows run by the OCADU students who studied in Florence, IT. One show in Florence and another a year later in the main gallery of OCADU.
